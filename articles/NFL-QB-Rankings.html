NFL QB Rankings: Full Breakdown & Player Analysis

Our latest NFL quarterback rankings with tiers, stats, and player-by-player analysis from the elite to the biggest question marks.

Quarterback play continues to define the NFL. In a league built on explosive offenses and split-second decisions, the difference between good and great often comes down to who’s under center. From established superstars to rising young talent, the quarterback landscape is as competitive as it’s ever been.

With that in mind, we’ve put together our latest quarterback rankings. We are factoring in performance, consistency, supporting cast, and overall impact on winning. Some names are obvious; others might spark debate.

Tier 1 — Elite

1. Josh Allen | Buffalo Bills

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 3668 | Pass TD: 25 | INT: 10 | Rush Yds: 579 | Rush TD: 14

Josh Allen is an elite dual-threat quarterback, combining strong passing numbers with over 550 yards rushing and 14 TDs. He was one of the most dangerous red-zone weapons in football, consistently extending drives with his mobility and finishing drives with his legs. His durability and continued improved efficiency made him one of the most reliable weekly performers. He ranks at the top because he runs an offense in a way no other QB can.

2. Patrick Mahomes | Kansas City Chiefs

Stats: Games: 14 | Pass Yds: 3587 | Pass TD: 22 | INT: 11 | Rush Yds: 422 | Rush TD: 5

Even in a down year, Mahomes’ efficiency and game control remained elite. His added rushing impact kept defenses off balance and extended plays consistently. He remained the most reliable quarterback in high-leverage situations. Despite his TD:INT ratio taking a hit this season and missing time, he ranks 2nd on this list because of his ability to elevate the offense and his teammates beyond what just the numbers can tell you.

Tier 2 — Wish You Had Them

3. Matthew Stafford | Los Angeles Rams

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4707 | Pass TD: 46 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 1 | Rush TD: 0

MVP Matthew Stafford was the most productive pure passer in the league, consistently pushing the ball downfield and finishing drives through the air. He is the last of a dying breed as his ability to run the offense comes singularly from the pocket. What he lacks in mobility he makes up for with his ability to navigate the pocket and execute under pressure. With a nearly 6:1 TD to INT ratio, he ranks this high because his passing production and scoring output were simply too great to ignore.

4. Joe Burrow | Cincinnati Bengals

Stats: Games: 8 | Pass Yds: 1809 | Pass TD: 17 | INT: 5 | Rush Yds: 41 | Rush TD: 0

Joe Burrow was highly efficient when on the field; unfortunately, he wasn’t on the field much. When playing, he is one of the most accurate passers with excellent decision-making that keeps the offense operating at a high level. However, availability is a big factor, and missing over half the season significantly impacts his overall grade. His ranking takes a hit because he simply did not play enough.

5. Lamar Jackson | Baltimore Ravens

Stats: Games: 13 | Pass Yds: 2549 | Pass TD: 21 | INT: 7 | Rush Yds: 349 | Rush TD: 2

Lamar Jackson is the epitome of a dual-threat QB, who has completely changed the game. Combining efficient passing with his rushing upside, his impact goes beyond the stats. His mobility consistently creates stress on defenses and opens the offense. His rushing totals dipped though and playing only 13 games this season limited his overall ranking. He ranks within the top 5 because he was very good, but not quite at his peak dominance.

Tier 3 — Breakouts & Stat Monsters

6. Drake Maye | New England Patriots

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4394 | Pass TD: 31 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 450 | Rush TD: 4

Maye’s poise and playmaking were impressive for a second-year QB, consistently leading his team on scoring drives. His athletic ability was on full display, showing true dual-threat capabilities. However, a weaker strength of schedule raises some questions about how much the production was boosted. He ranks highly for the elite numbers, but context keeps him just below the higher tiers. Can he do it again in 2026?

7. Dak Prescott | Dallas Cowboys

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4552 | Pass TD: 30 | INT: 10 | Rush Yds: 177 | Rush TD: 2

Dak Prescott delivered elite passing production with 4,552 yards and 30 TDs while keeping turnovers in check. He consistently drove one of the league’s most productive offenses through accuracy and timing. However, he offers limited rushing impact compared to dual-threat QBs above him. He ranks highly because of top-tier passing statistics, just below the overall impact of the higher tier.

8. Jared Goff | Detroit Lions

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4564 | Pass TD: 34 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 45 | Rush TD: 0

Jared Goff operated one of the league’s most efficient offenses, consistently executing at a high level within the structure of his offense. His playmakers are elite, but he is putting everyone in position to do their job and succeed. He operates in the pocket well, but his limited rushing upside and lack of creativity to extend plays hold him back from being higher on this list. With the utmost respect possible, he is the ultimate game manager.

9. Jalen Hurts | Philadelphia Eagles

Stats: Games: 16 | Pass Yds: 3224 | Pass TD: 25 | INT: 6 | Rush Yds: 421 | Rush TD: 8

Hurts remained a high-impact dual-threat QB, throwing 25 TDs and adding 8 rushing TDs. His ability to finish drives on the ground makes him one of the most dangerous red-zone players in the league. His passing volume and overall yardage trail the top-tier QBs. He ranks here because of strong scoring impact, but slightly less total offensive production.

Tier 4 — Productive but Flawed

10. Trevor Lawrence | Jacksonville Jaguars

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4007 | Pass TD: 29 | INT: 12 | Rush Yds: 359 | Rush TD: 9

Trevor Lawrence produced strong all-around numbers with 4,007 passing yards and 29 TDs while adding 9 rushing TDs. His dual-threat ability helped drive consistent scoring and offensive balance. However, 12 interceptions and stretches of inconsistency limit his efficiency. He ranks here because of solid production but lacks the control and ceiling of the top tier.

11. Brock Purdy | San Francisco 49ers

Stats: Games: 9 | Pass Yds: 2167 | Pass TD: 20 | INT: 10 | Rush Yds: 147 | Rush TD: 3

Brock Purdy was highly productive when available, throwing for 20 TDs in 9 games. He showed efficiency and playmaking ability within the offense, consistently finishing drives with touchdowns. A high turnover rate and missing time limited his overall grade. He ranks here because of high-level production when healthy, but he lacked consistency and availability.

12. Baker Mayfield | Tampa Bay Buccaneers

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 3693 | Pass TD: 26 | INT: 11 | Rush Yds: 382 | Rush TD: 1

Baker Mayfield delivered solid, well-rounded production through the air while surprisingly adding value on the ground. He played solid football and kept the offense moving without major volatility. He doesn’t dominate in any one area compared to higher-ranked QBs. He ranks here because of his solid performance, but he does lack elite upside and game-changing ability.

13. Justin Herbert | Los Angeles Chargers

Stats: Games: 16 | Pass Yds: 3727 | Pass TD: 26 | INT: 13 | Rush Yds: 498 | Rush TD: 2

Justin Herbert produced strong overall numbers with 3,727 passing yards and 26 TDs while adding 498 rushing yards. His arm talent and mobility create consistent explosive play potential. However, 13 interceptions and inconsistency limited his efficiency. He ranks here because of high upside, but less control and consistency than the tier above him.

14. Jordan Love | Green Bay Packers

Stats: Games: 15 | Pass Yds: 3381 | Pass TD: 23 | INT: 6 | Rush Yds: 199 | Rush TD: 0

Jordan Love combined strong efficiency (23 TDs, 6 INTs) with steady command of the offense. He played within structure and consistently avoided costly mistakes. He ranks here because of efficiency but lacks the production of higher-tier QBs.

Tier 5 — Young Talent

15. Caleb Williams | Chicago Bears

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 3942 | Pass TD: 27 | INT: 7 | Rush Yds: 388 | Rush TD: 3

Caleb Williams delivered a strong 2nd season, showing steady improvement over his rookie year. His ability to create both in and out of structure gave the offense consistent explosiveness. Keeping his eyes downfield while navigating out of the pocket and showing rare athleticism on his throws really sets him apart. There were still moments of inconsistency and head-scratching throws, which shows as he had the lowest completion percentage of qualified QBs. He ranks here because of high-level production and upside, but he is still developing as a true passer and top-tier QB.

16. Jayden Daniels | Washington Commanders

Stats: Games: 7 | Pass Yds: 1262 | Pass TD: 8 | INT: 3 | Rush Yds: 278 | Rush TD: 2

Jayden Daniels flashed high-end dual-threat ability, adding 278 rushing yards in just 7 games. His mobility and playmaking ability created explosive moments and extended drives. However, limited sample size and developing passing production cap his impact and grade. He ranks here because of upside, and what he demonstrated his rookie season.

17. Bo Nix | Denver Broncos

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 3931 | Pass TD: 25 | INT: 11 | Rush Yds: 356 | Rush TD: 5

Bo Nix delivered strong all-around production both with passing and demonstrating strong rushing upside. He showed good command of the offense and was able to extend drives with his pocket awareness and mobility. Eleven interceptions and some inconsistency limit his efficiency. He ranks here because of balanced production but isn’t as polished as the tier above him.

Tier 6 — Capable Starters

18. Sam Darnold | Seattle Seahawks

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 4048 | Pass TD: 25 | INT: 14 | Rush Yds: 95 | Rush TD: 0

Super Bowl-winning QB Sam Darnold put together a career year with over 4,000 passing yards and 25 TDs. He showed that ’24 with the Vikings was not a fluke and was able to lead this offense, consistently putting his team in position to win. He demonstrated that he is a QB you can win with with the right complementing parts. He doesn’t rank as high as a Super Bowl-winning QB might, due to his high turnover rate and limited athleticism compared to his peers. Solid production, limited upside, proven to be a player you can win it all with.

19. Aaron Rodgers | Pittsburgh Steelers

Stats: Games: 16 | Pass Yds: 3322 | Pass TD: 24 | INT: 7 | Rush Yds: 61 | Rush TD: 1

Veteran Aaron Rodgers delivered an efficient season with 24 TDs to just 7 INTs, bouncing back from a season with the Jets. He managed the offense well and limited mistakes, showing strong decision-making. However, his lack of mobility and lower overall production reduce his playmaking impact. His mind is still top tier; he just isn’t what he used to be physically at 43 years old. He ranks here because he can still run an efficient, well-run offense, but no longer can provide the elite-level talent we had known from him at this stage in his career.

Tier 7 — Need to Put It All Together

20. Jaxson Dart | New York Giants

Stats: Games: 14 | Pass Yds: 2272 | Pass TD: 15 | INT: 5 | Rush Yds: 487 | Rush TD: 9

Jaxson Dart, appearing in 14 games but only starting 12, made a major impact on the ground with 487 rushing yards and 9 TDs, giving his offense a true dual-threat element. His mobility consistently created opportunities and extended drives. Performances against the toughest schedule in the NFL last season did not go unnoticed. However, his passing ability remains a work in progress, especially on the deep ball. He ranks here because of elite rushing impact, but he is still developing as a passer.

21. Daniel Jones | Indianapolis Colts

Stats: Games: 13 | Pass Yds: 3101 | Pass TD: 19 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 164 | Rush TD: 5

Daniel Jones provided solid overall production with 3,101 passing yards and 19 TDs while adding 5 rushing TDs. His mobility gave the offense an added dimension and helped in scoring situations. However, his passing impact remained average and lacked consistent explosiveness. He ranks here because of balanced production, but limited upside compared to higher-tier QBs.

22. Kyler Murray | Minnesota Vikings

Stats: Games: 5 | Pass Yds: 962 | Pass TD: 6 | INT: 3 | Rush Yds: 173 | Rush TD: 1

Kyler Murray showed solid dual-threat ability in limited action, producing through both the air and on the ground. His mobility and playmaking still create problems for defenses when he’s on the field. However, playing only 5 games significantly limits his overall impact and evaluation. He ranks here because of ability, but availability holds him back.

23. C.J. Stroud | Houston Texans

Stats: Games: 14 | Pass Yds: 3041 | Pass TD: 19 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 209 | Rush TD: 1

C.J. Stroud delivered steady regular-season production with 3,041 yards and 19 TDs while limiting mistakes. He showed solid command of the offense and consistent decision-making. However, his poor playoff performance raised concerns about his ability to elevate in high-pressure moments. He ranks here because while consistent, he lacked both elite production and postseason impact.

Tier 8 — What We Have Seen

24. Cam Ward | Tennessee Titans

Stats: Games: 17 | Pass Yds: 3169 | Pass TD: 15 | INT: 7 | Rush Yds: 159 | Rush TD: 2

Cam Ward’s rookie season is hard to evaluate. Being on one of the worst rosters in the NFL, he flashed playmaking ability but struggled to consistently convert drives into touchdowns. His overall efficiency and impact lagged behind most starting QBs. He ranks here because the production didn’t translate to high-end results; his career is young though and there is still plenty of potential.

25. Tyler Shough | New Orleans Saints

Stats: Games: 11 | Pass Yds: 2384 | Pass TD: 10 | INT: 6 | Rush Yds: 186 | Rush TD: 3

Tyler Shough showed flashes in his 11 games, starting 9. He added some mobility, but overall production remained limited. His impact was inconsistent, with struggles sustaining drives and generating scoring. Although his team achieved a commendable 5-4 record with him under center, it is important to note that the opposing teams they defeated were not particularly strong. He ranks here because we need to see more to truly evaluate him as a QB.

26. Malik Willis | Miami Dolphins

Stats: Games: 4 | Pass Yds: 422 | Pass TD: 3 | INT: 0 | Rush Yds: 123 | Rush TD: 2

Malik Willis showed flashes in limited action in his time with the Packers. His one start last season was against Baltimore, which resulted in a commanding win, and it may have been what earned him his 3-year, $67.5 million contract with the Dolphins. He has had time to develop under a great offensive mind in Matt LaFleur. We have seen resurrection stories from QBs before, and his mobility adds a dynamic element to his game, so his ranking is based on what we have seen so far, not what he could become.

27. Jacoby Brissett | Arizona Cardinals

Stats: Games: 14 | Pass Yds: 3366 | Pass TD: 23 | INT: 8 | Rush Yds: 168 | Rush TD: 1

Brissett delivered solid efficiency with 23 TDs to 8 INTs and 3,366 passing yards. He managed the offense well and avoided major mistakes. However, he offered limited playmaking and didn’t elevate the offense beyond structure. He ranks here because of steady production, but lacks high-end impact.

28. Bryce Young | Carolina Panthers

Stats: Games: 16 | Pass Yds: 3011 | Pass TD: 23 | INT: 11 | Rush Yds: 216 | Rush TD: 2

Bryce Young showed improvement with 3,011 passing yards and 23 TDs while adding some mobility. He managed the offense better and reduced major mistakes compared to earlier struggles. However, inconsistency and efficiency are still things he needs to improve on. He ranks here because he hasn’t provided enough of an impact to move up.

29. Michael Penix Jr. | Atlanta Falcons

Stats: Games: 9 | Pass Yds: 1982 | Pass TD: 9 | INT: 3 | Rush Yds: 70 | Rush TD: 1

Michael Penix Jr. has strong arm talent and the ability to push the ball downfield. He just has not found it yet at the NFL level, completing 60.1% of his passes and averaging 220 yards through the air per game. His low touchdown rate, lack of mobility, inability to stay healthy, and limited overall production determined his rank.

Tier 9 — Wait and See

30. Fernando Mendoza | Las Vegas Raiders

2025 College Stats: Games: 16 | Pass Yds: 3535 | Pass TD: 41 | INT: 6 | Rush Yds: 276 | Rush TD: 7

Fernando Mendoza projects as a high-upside pocket passer whose elite efficiency and touchdown production translate well to modern NFL offenses built on timing and anticipation. His biggest strength is accuracy at all three levels, but he’ll need to prove he can consistently handle tighter NFL throwing windows and faster defensive processing. He does have mobility but doesn’t overly rely on his legs, so his success will hinge on decision-making, pocket presence, and scheme fit.

Tier 10 — Struggles

31. Geno Smith | New York Jets

Stats: Games: 15 | Pass Yds: 3025 | Pass TD: 19 | INT: 17 | Rush Yds: 177 | Rush TD: 2

Geno struggled with consistency and decision-making throughout the 2025 season. While he could move the offense at times, holding onto the ball too long and turnovers often stalled drives and flipped momentum. He ranks here because when you lead your team to the first overall pick you get replaced; worse for Geno though, he got sent to the Jets.

32. Deshaun Watson | Cleveland Browns

Stats: Games: 0

At this point, projecting Deshaun Watson as anything more than a question mark is optimistic given his extended time away. The Browns, though, haven’t made their thoughts clear yet on if they will give Watson the opportunity to start next season or go with one of their 2025 draft picks. Even if healthy next season and given the starting nod, there are serious concerns about rust, consistency, and whether he can return anywhere near his previous level. Good luck.
